As part of the APO series, the Navier–Stokes documents seek to answer why flows are information transporters, and how we can characterize the information within the flow. Information geometry has an opinion on what it means to have a defined flow, and that requires distinguishability. 

It is related to the dynamics of information, velocities, trajectories, time evolution, and history of flow (ergodicity). We show that all fluids have it, and that the NS problem gives a fertile ground to ask such questions. We discover that NS flows are Turing Machines, and can encode unbounded computational power in blow-up singularities. The flow simulates a Turing Complete universal computer, which also encodes the halting problem. Because it encodes it, we hit a computational universality wall, a Church-Turing wall, due to facing the halting problem, as no computer can decide halting.
